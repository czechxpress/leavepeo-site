5 Numbers to Pull from Your PEO Invoice Before Your Next Renewal

Most companies renew on autopilot. Run these five calculations before you sign another year.

Peter Lehr  •  IMA Financial Group  •  March 2026

Most companies renew their PEO contract because switching feels complicated. That is not a good reason to stay. Before you sign another year, pull these five numbers from your invoice. What you find may change your decision entirely.

Number 1

Your Effective Administrative Rate as a Percentage of Total Payroll

Your PEO charges either a flat per-employee-per-month (PEPM) fee or a percentage of payroll. Either way, convert it into one clean number so you can benchmark it against the market.

The math: Divide your total annual PEO administrative fees — not including actual insurance premiums or payroll taxes — by your total annual gross payroll. Multiply by 100. That is your effective admin rate.

Industry range: 3% to 12% of total gross payroll. Administrative fees typically run $40 to $160 per employee per month. If you are consistently above 8% and you are not in a high-complexity, multi-state environment, you have room to negotiate or a concrete reason to shop.

Most business owners have never run this number. Their PEO has been content to keep it that way.

Number 2

The Markup on Your Health Insurance Premium

This is the number most PEOs least want you to calculate. Ask your PEO for the actual carrier invoice — the document that shows what the insurance company charges at the source. Then compare it to what your PEO is billing you.

The gap between those two figures is margin. It is not always disclosed, and there is no legal requirement that it be. Some PEOs bundle insurance costs with hidden administrative markups, making it genuinely difficult to understand how much you are paying for healthcare versus how much you are paying the PEO to process it.

Without clear line-item invoices, businesses can unknowingly overspend by thousands of dollars per year on health coverage alone. If your PEO cannot or will not produce the underlying carrier invoice, that is your answer.
Number 3

Your Workers' Comp Rate vs. Your Actual Claims History

Inside a PEO, your workers' compensation is pooled with their entire book of business. That arrangement makes sense when you are small and your own loss history is thin. As your company grows and accumulates clean claims history, the calculus changes.

Companies with few or no claims over three-plus years often qualify for standalone workers' comp policies at meaningfully lower rates than they pay inside the pool. Your PEO has no incentive to tell you this.

Action step: Pull your OSHA 300 log and claims history for the past three years. Take that to a standalone workers' comp carrier or an independent broker. Compare that quote against what you currently pay inside the PEO. The gap is often substantial — particularly for professional services, technology, and office-based businesses.
Number 4

What You Are Paying Per Employee Per Year, All-In

Take your total annual PEO invoice. Subtract all actual pass-through costs at face value — payroll taxes, benefits premiums at their true carrier cost, and any other direct costs that belong to your employees rather than your PEO. Divide what remains by your headcount.

That is your all-in administrative cost per employee per year.

The national average for PEO administrative fees is approximately $1,395 per employee per year according to NAPEO. If your number is materially higher and you are not dealing with complex multi-state compliance or high-risk industry exposure, you are worth a second opinion.

This calculation forces transparency. Most PEO invoices are deliberately structured to make this number hard to find. Do the math anyway.

Number 5

Your Exit Penalty and Renewal Window

Pull your PEO agreement right now and find two things: the exit penalty clause and the renewal notification deadline.

Most PEO contracts include fees for leaving before the contract term ends. More critically, most auto-renew — and the window to opt out is typically 30 to 60 days before your anniversary date. Miss that window and you are locked in for another full year regardless of what you discover about your pricing.

Put the opt-out deadline in your calendar today, regardless of whether you plan to leave. Knowing your window gives you negotiating leverage even if you ultimately decide to stay. Your PEO's renewal conversation will look very different once they know you have done this work.

What to Do With These Numbers

If any of these figures look off, you are not obligated to sort it out alone.
